title: anatomy of melancholy
---

love, writes robert burton, is "a species of melancholy", an "incurable disease"; this brutish sort of love to which he referred, unlike charity (whose virtues are drawn from piety and friendship), tyrannizes us and works upon our soul "through the eye", which are termed, endearingly, the "harbingers of love"; no, says he, this love is rather a "fascination", for we are bewitched by our lover's sight, enamored by its lustre, and drawn to the "secret lodestone" of their beauty; in sum, he continues, this love is a sleight of hand, a "kind of legerdemain" that ever confounds us - it is at once fire, ice, cold, and hot; a "rainbow of all colours", he concludes, alluding to its constant alterations, its symptoms so numerous as to be incomprehensible by a single mind; this love is "a plague, a torture, a hell, a bittersweet passion", and breeds grief as much as joy, for (remember) it is the son of mars and venus, delighting both in pleasure and in war; therefore we are justified in saying that a "grievous wound is love still", and that a lover's heart is "cupid's quiver"; out of all the passions, love is deemed by him the most violent, because from love (he says, borrowing from augustine) arises enmities, sorrow, and discontent, as well as quarrels and ever-flowing tears; and yet love is dimensionless, for no art or engine can fathom it; it is, at last, a "bottomless pit"; no water can quench it, no fire consume it, no other pleasure can distract us from its grasp; as long as we love, we are at perpetual mercy of another's whims, and can only find peace at rare intervals, when we are granted a glimpse of our lover's face, or a hint of their perfume, or a taste of their kiss; then our spirits are lifted up again, and we count ourselves among the gods, while we wait for the wind to blow the other way.
